## 1. Introduction and Purpose

Vertex Metals Ltd ("the Company") is a commodity and metals sourcing intermediary incorporated on the Isle of Man under the Companies Act 1931. The Company acts as principal in trades, sourcing metals, metal products, critical minerals, and mineral concentrates from qualified international suppliers and supplying UK and European industrial buyers under documented contracts. The Company does not hold physical stock on its own account; title passes to the Company in transit and is onward-sold to the qualified buyer against agreed trade terms.

This policy establishes the Company's framework for complying with its anti-money laundering (AML), counter-terrorist financing (CTF), and customer due diligence (CDD) obligations. It sets out:

- The regulatory framework the Company operates within
- The Company's risk assessment approach
- The due diligence standards applied to suppliers and to buyers
- The credit framework governing post-graduation commercial terms
- The sanctions screening requirements
- The ESG and responsible-sourcing commitments
- The ongoing monitoring, record-keeping, reporting, and training obligations
- The governance and approval requirements for commercial and compliance decisions

This policy replaces Version 1.0 in full. It reflects the Company's operating model as a multi-geography, multi-product sourcing desk rather than a single-product importer, and incorporates credit-framework and commercial-terms provisions that Version 1.0 did not contain.

This policy is prepared in accordance with:

- The Proceeds of Crime Act 2008 (Isle of Man)
- The Anti-Terrorism and Crime Act 2003 (Isle of Man)
- The Anti-Money Laundering and Countering the Financing of Terrorism Code 2019 (Isle of Man)
- The Designated Businesses (Registration and Oversight) Act 2015 (Isle of Man)
- The Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (United Kingdom, as amended)
- UK financial sanctions legislation and Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) reporting obligations
- Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Recommendations applicable to Designated Non-Financial Businesses and Professions
- The UK National Risk Assessment of Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing, current edition
- Relevant guidance issued by the Isle of Man Financial Services Authority

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to:

- All directors, officers, and persons acting on behalf of Vertex Metals Ltd
- All business relationships with suppliers, including producers, mills, smelters, and trading counterparties
- All business relationships with buyers, including industrial manufacturers, distributors, and other commercial purchasers

### 4.2 United Kingdom — trading operations

The Company trades into the United Kingdom as Importer of Record for goods sold to UK buyers. The Company's trading operations are subject to:

- UK Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (as amended) — in respect of the Company's obligations as an Importer of Record conducting high-value commercial trade
- UK financial sanctions regime administered by OFSI — applicable to all trade and counterparty activity with a UK nexus

The Company will register as a High Value Dealer immediately should any change in settlement practice trigger the requirement.

### 4.4 OFSI reporting

The Company is a relevant firm for the purposes of UK financial sanctions obligations. The Company reports to OFSI at HM Treasury where it has evidence, or reasonable grounds to suspect, that a person it deals with has breached or is connected to a breach of financial sanctions regulations. ## 5. Business Risk Assessment

### 5.1 Approach

The Company conducts a business-wide risk assessment to identify and assess the money laundering, terrorist financing, proliferation financing, and sanctions risks arising from its operations. The assessment considers counterparty risk, geography risk, product risk, transaction-structure risk, and delivery-channel risk.

The assessment is reviewed annually, on any material change to the business, and following any suspicious activity report or compliance incident. The current assessment is recorded in a separate working document; a summary is set out below and is updated as the Company's business evolves.

### 5.2 Geography risk

The Company sources globally. Country-level risk is assessed with reference to:

- FATF public statements identifying high-risk and monitored jurisdictions
- The UK National Risk Assessment
- OFSI, UN, and EU sanctions designations
- Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index
- Specific sectoral risks (e.g. conflict minerals in the African Great Lakes region)

The Company's sourcing geographies are categorised for operational purposes as follows. This categorisation is indicative, is updated in line with the published sources above, and is not a substitute for counterparty-level due diligence.

**Standard-risk geographies:** the United Kingdom; European Economic Area; India; South Korea; Japan; Taiwan; Australia; Canada; Chile; Singapore.

**Elevated-risk geographies (Enhanced Due Diligence applies):** China; Turkey; United Arab Emirates; any other jurisdiction where FATF monitoring, sanctions nexus, or sectoral risk indicators warrant additional scrutiny at the time of the relationship being assessed.

**High-risk or restricted geographies (transaction not permitted, or requires specific MLRO and Board approval with documented justification):** any jurisdiction subject to comprehensive UK, UN, or EU sanctions; any FATF black-listed jurisdiction; any jurisdiction where the Company's products would face realistic risk of sanctions-evasion use.

### 5.3 Product risk

The Company's product categories are assessed for inherent risk as follows:

**Standard risk:** commercial metal products in finished or semi-finished form (bars, plates, coils, rods, pipes, sheets, tubes, channel, beams, profiles, wire) in aluminium, copper, stainless steel, brass, bronze, and tool steel. These products have well-established commercial markets, verifiable origins, and limited dual-use concerns.

**Elevated risk:** titanium and titanium alloys (export controls; dual-use potential); nickel superalloys including Inconel grades (defence and aerospace applications); specific specialist alloys on request. Enhanced Due Diligence applies to counterparties and, where relevant, end-use verification is required.

**Elevated risk — responsible sourcing:** mica (child-labour concerns in unregulated artisanal sourcing); cobalt and cobalt-adjacent materials (DRC-sourcing concerns); any material with documented ESG supply-chain concerns. Mandatory responsible-sourcing certification applies (see section 12 and the separate Responsible Sourcing Policy).

**Elevated risk — sanctions-adjacent:** copper (Russian-origin restrictions); aluminium (Russian-origin restrictions from specific dates); any material where sanctions-origin evasion is a documented pattern. Origin verification is mandatory; sanctions screening applies to the full supply chain to the extent verifiable.

**Elevated risk — CBAM:** aluminium (from 1 January 2027); other in-scope materials as CBAM is expanded. CBAM readiness applies (see section 13).

**Forward-capability products (Critical minerals and mineral concentrates):** copper concentrate; lithium carbonate and lithium battery precursors; manganese ore; chromite and chromium; bauxite. These are not part of day-one active inventory but are marketed as a sourcing capability. Each enquiry is assessed case-by-case; the policy applies in full from the point of first engagement with any specific supplier.

### 5.4 Counterparty risk

Each supplier and buyer is risk-rated individually at onboarding and on each periodic review. Factors considered include ownership and control structure, PEP status, adverse media, sanctions proximity, financial standing, historical trading conduct, and sector-specific risk indicators.

### 5.5 Transaction-structure risk

Transactions are settled by bank transfer or Irrevocable Documentary Letter of Credit only. The Company uses straight back-to-back Letter of Credit structures, with independent Letters of Credit on the buyer and supplier sides of any trade. The Company does not use transferable Letters of Credit with document-substitution ("ITDLC") mechanics, and does not operate "switch Bill of Lading" arrangements designed to conceal origin. Information-siloing mechanisms of this kind are not part of the Company's operating model.

Any request by a counterparty for structures intended to obscure the identity of the other side of the trade, the origin of goods, the beneficial ownership of funds, or the routing of payments is a red flag and is escalated to the MLRO. The commercial legitimacy of protecting the Company's supplier–buyer relationship through contractual means is separate from, and does not justify, documentary concealment.

### 5.6 Overall risk rating

The Company's inherent risk is assessed as **medium**. Following application of the controls in this policy — tiered CDD, mandatory sanctions screening, no-cash settlement, on-site supplier audit, MLRO approval gates, and ongoing monitoring — the residual risk is assessed as **low-to-medium**. This rating is reviewed at each annual policy review.

## 6. Customer Due Diligence — Buyers

### 6.1 Principle

No order is accepted from a new buyer, and no first trade is executed, until Customer Due Diligence has been completed and approved by the MLRO. This requirement is absolute and applies regardless of order urgency, order size, or the existence of a prior relationship with any party involved in the trade.

### 6.2 Standard CDD — UK and standard-risk-geography buyers

The following information is obtained and independently verified before first order:

| Item | Acceptable verification |
|---|---|
| Legal name and registration number | Companies House (UK) or equivalent official register |
| Registered and trading address | Official register; corroborated by independent source (e.g. utility bill, bank statement, website) |
| Directors and beneficial owners (25%+) | PSC register (UK); equivalent in other jurisdictions; supplemented by identity verification of key individuals |
| Nature and purpose of business | Written statement; corroborated by company website and independent sources |
| Nature and purpose of the business relationship | Written statement from the buyer covering intended products, expected volumes, intended frequency, and stated commercial profile including any scale-up pathway (section 8.5) |
| Sanctions screening — all directors and UBOs | UK (OFSI), UN, EU sanctions lists; documented date, source, and result |
| PEP screening — all directors and UBOs | PEP database; adverse media check; documented result |
| Financial standing | Credit report; most recent published accounts where available; bank reference for first credit-terms graduation |
| Confirmed payment method | Written acknowledgement that payment is by bank transfer or LC only; cash is not accepted |
| VAT and EORI status | Verified for UK buyers; equivalent for non-UK buyers as relevant |

### 6.3 Enhanced Due Diligence — buyers

Enhanced Due Diligence applies where any of the following is present:

- The buyer is based in or connected to an elevated-risk or high-risk geography (section 5.2)
- The buyer's beneficial ownership is complex, layered through multiple jurisdictions, or includes corporate or trust vehicles without clear economic rationale
- Any director or UBO is a Politically Exposed Person, or closely connected to one
- Adverse media is identified concerning the buyer, its directors, or its UBOs
- The buyer's stated business activity does not match the nature or scale of the proposed transaction
- The first order is materially above the Company's standard first-order profile for the relevant product, or the stated onboarding profile indicates a scale-up pathway above £200,000 aggregate annual trade (section 8.5)
- The buyer has requested or indicated non-standard transaction structures, third-party payments, or unusual payment routing
- Any other factor identified by the MLRO as warranting enhanced scrutiny

EDD requires, in addition to standard CDD:

- Source of funds verification for the proposed trade (not source of wealth, which is a separate question relevant primarily to individual UBOs)
- Verified information on the buyer's end-use of the goods, where product risk warrants it
- MLRO approval before the relationship is established, recorded in the buyer file
- Enhanced ongoing monitoring, with more frequent review cycles

### 6.4 Simplified Due Diligence — buyers

Simplified Due Diligence is rarely appropriate for the Company's business and is not applied by default. Where the MLRO considers SDD may be justified (for example, in the case of a UK-listed company or regulated entity whose public disclosures already satisfy the substance of CDD), the decision is documented with reasoning in the buyer file and reviewed at the next annual policy review.

### 6.5 First-order protocol (pro-forma settlement)

All first orders from new buyers are processed on a pro-forma basis, regardless of CDD outcome or buyer financial strength. The first-order settlement structure is:

- **Option A:** 100% payment received before the Company commits to the supplier
- **Option B:** deposit (typically 40%, specifically agreed per buyer and order) received before the Company commits to the supplier, with the balance secured by Irrevocable Documentary Letter of Credit issued by a reputable bank, payable on shipping documents or at net+15 / net+30 post-delivery

The Company does not commit to a supplier on a first order until the pro-forma payment (or deposit plus confirmed LC) is in place.

### 6.6 Graduation to credit terms

A buyer becomes eligible to be considered for credit terms on future orders only where all of the following apply:

- At minimum one successfully completed trade on first-order protocol, with no payment default, payment delay, dispute, or other commercial concern
- Refreshed financial and credit-standing review
- Refreshed sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media screening
- Supplier-side willingness to align on credit terms for the trade in question
- MLRO and Director approval, recorded in the buyer file

Graduation is not automatic after the first trade. In practice the number of successfully completed pro-forma trades before graduation may be more than one, depending on the size and pattern of trading and the MLRO's judgment. Where a buyer's onboarding captured a specific scale-up pathway (section 8.5), graduation may be structured against that pathway at a pre-agreed point.

## 7. Supplier Due Diligence

### 7.1 Principle

No order is placed with a new supplier until Supplier Due Diligence has been completed and the supplier has been added to the Approved Supplier List by the MLRO. No supplier outside the Approved Supplier List may be transacted with, irrespective of commercial pressure or buyer request.

### 7.2 Standard Supplier DD

The following information is obtained and verified before approval:

- Certificate of incorporation and company registration documents from the jurisdiction of incorporation
- Directors and beneficial owners (25%+) with identity verification of principal individuals
- Relevant trade and export authorisations (for example, IEC from DGFT for Indian suppliers; equivalents elsewhere)
- Nature and location of production facilities, manufacturing methods, and product capability
- Production capacity, typical lead times, and committed volumes to other customers
- Sanctions screening — entity and principals — against UK, UN, EU lists
- Adverse media screening on the company and its principals
- Bank account details in the name of the supplier entity (third-party payment arrangements are not accepted)
- Tax registration, audit status, and financial standing to the extent available
- Certifications relevant to product quality (ISO 9001, mill certificates, grade specifications) and to responsible sourcing (see section 12 and the Responsible Sourcing Policy)
- Carbon intensity and energy-source information for CBAM-relevant products (section 13)

### 7.3 Enhanced Supplier DD

Enhanced Due Diligence applies where any of the following is present:

- The supplier is based in or connected to an elevated-risk or high-risk geography (section 5.2)
- The supplier produces elevated-risk products (section 5.3)
- Complex ownership or control structure
- PEP or adverse-media nexus
- Financial or operational indicators suggesting stress
- Any factor identified by the MLRO as warranting enhanced scrutiny

EDD requires the standard DD items plus additional source-of-funds verification, enhanced ownership tracing, verification of regulatory standing in the home jurisdiction, and MLRO approval before approval.

### 7.4 On-site audit — new suppliers

Before a new supplier is added to the Approved Supplier List, the Company conducts an on-site audit of the supplier's production facility. The purpose of the audit is to verify that:

- The facility exists and operates as described in the supplier documentation
- Production methods, capacity, and quality systems are consistent with documented claims
- Working conditions and health-and-safety practices meet the Company's minimum standards
- Responsible-sourcing controls for relevant materials are in place and evidenced
- There are no obvious indicators of sanctions-evasion routing, re-origination of goods, or similar patterns

Audits are carried out by a director of the Company, by a trusted appointed agent (once engaged), or by an accepted third-party auditor (for example SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek) where a recent independent audit report is available and is specific to the facility in question. Where practical, multiple supplier audits in the same geography are clustered into a single visit for cost efficiency.

### 7.5 Grandfathered suppliers

Suppliers with whom Jackson Paul has an established trading history from his prior sole-proprietor metals trading activity may be onboarded as "grandfathered" suppliers on the following basis:

- Full fresh AML/KYC is performed in the name of Vertex Metals Ltd regardless of prior history
- Sanctions and adverse-media screening is fresh
- Financial and operational information is refreshed
- The historical trading relationship is documented in the supplier file, with reference to the years of prior dealings, the volume and pattern of prior trade, and any known issues
- An immediate on-site audit is not required as a condition of initial approval, provided the historical relationship satisfies the MLRO as sufficient alternative assurance
- An on-site audit is conducted within twelve months of first Vertex Metals trade with the supplier, and thereafter on the standard two-year cycle

Grandfathered status does not reduce the standard of ongoing monitoring, sanctions screening, or responsible-sourcing requirements.

### 7.6 Ongoing supplier review

Approved suppliers are subject to:

- Sanctions and adverse-media re-screening every six months and on any new relevant designation
- Annual document refresh (registration, authorisations, financial information, certifications)
- On-site re-audit every two years, or sooner where risk indicators warrant

Any material adverse change — sanctions proximity, ownership change, financial distress, quality incident, responsible-sourcing concern — triggers an immediate MLRO review and, where warranted, suspension or removal from the Approved Supplier List.

### 7.7 Buyer requests concerning suppliers

Buyers may request continuity with a supplier previously used on their prior orders, on the basis of established quality and fit. Such requests are accommodated where the supplier remains on the Approved Supplier List and the commercial circumstances permit.

Buyers may not nominate a specific supplier not already on the Approved Supplier List. Where a buyer indicates a preference for a supplier not currently approved, this is treated as a request for the Company to consider adding that supplier to the Approved List. The supplier is subject to the full supplier due diligence process, including on-site audit where required, before any commitment is made. The Company reserves the discretion to decline to onboard any supplier regardless of buyer preference, and the Company does not accept buyer instruction on which supplier to use for any given order.

Where a buyer's request to use a specific supplier is accompanied by indicators suggesting a pre-existing relationship between buyer and supplier, or a commercial rationale that would normally support a direct purchase, Enhanced Due Diligence applies to both sides of the trade. The commercial rationale for the Company's intermediation is documented and approved by the MLRO before any onboarding step is taken.

## 8. Credit Framework

### 8.1 Principle

The Company extends commercial credit to buyers only following successful graduation from the first-order protocol, within documented credit limits, and subject to ongoing monitoring. Credit decisions are made against a written framework, not ad-hoc. Every credit decision is recorded in the buyer file with supporting rationale.

### 8.2 Initial credit limit on graduation

On graduation from first-order protocol to credit terms, the initial credit limit is set at the higher of:

- The value of the buyer's first successfully completed trade, or
- The pre-agreed scale-up profile value recorded at onboarding, where the buyer indicated at CDD stage that their first order was a trial ahead of a stated larger intended volume (section 8.5)

The initial credit limit is documented in the buyer file and communicated to the buyer in writing.

### 8.3 Cumulative outstanding limit

The buyer's credit limit applies on a cumulative basis. Total live exposure to the buyer — the sum of orders in transit plus any delivered-unpaid balances — may not exceed the credit limit at any point. Where a buyer wishes to place a further order that would take cumulative exposure above the limit, one of the following applies:

- The further order is delayed until earlier orders are settled and exposure is reduced
- The portion of the new order within the limit is processed on credit; the portion above the limit is processed on pro-forma or secured terms (split settlement, section 8.6)
- The credit limit is reviewed and, if warranted, adjusted upward in accordance with section 8.4

### 8.4 Review cycle

Credit limits are reviewed on the earlier of:

- Every three successfully completed trades on credit terms, or
- Every six months from the last review

Reviews consider payment history, trading volume, the buyer's ongoing financial standing, and any adverse information that has emerged. Limits may be increased, held, reduced, or (in cases of concern) suspended. Any increase above the initial limit requires MLRO and Director approval. A hard cap applies at three times the initial credit limit — increases above that multiple require Board approval and consideration of trade credit insurance.

### 8.5 Scale-up pathway at onboarding

Where a buyer indicates at CDD stage that their first order is a trial ahead of a larger intended volume (for example, a £20,000 test order ahead of intended £200,000 orders), the onboarding process captures:

- The stated larger intended volume
- The commercial rationale (buyer wishes to verify quality and process before committing)
- The buyer's financial capacity to support the stated larger volume — reviewed at onboarding, not deferred
- Sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media screening sized against the stated larger volume, not the test order value

The stated larger volume is recorded in the buyer file as the "pre-agreed scale-up profile" and forms the basis for the initial credit limit on graduation, subject to the trial trade being successfully completed.

### 8.6 Handling orders above the credit limit

A buyer on credit terms wishing to place an order that exceeds the established credit limit is accommodated through one of the following mechanisms, chosen commercially on a case-by-case basis:

- **Split settlement** — the portion up to the credit limit is processed on credit terms; the portion above it is processed on pro-forma or secured terms (deposit, LC, or other agreed security) for the first follow-up order. Subsequent orders of similar size may be eligible for full credit terms on review
- **Trade credit insurance** — the Company obtains credit insurance on the specific customer and order via a reputable trade credit insurer (for example, Atradius, Coface, Allianz Trade). Premium cost is factored into the commercial terms of the trade
- **Customer-provided security** — bank guarantee, parent-company guarantee (where the buyer is part of a larger group), standby Letter of Credit, or cash deposit for the portion of the order above the credit limit

Under no circumstances is an order above the credit limit accepted on unsecured credit terms without one of the above mechanisms in place.

### 8.7 Material scale-up beyond onboarding profile

Where a buyer seeks to place an order substantially above the commercial profile captured at onboarding — specifically, three times or more the stated scale-up profile or, if no scale-up profile was captured, three times the largest previously completed trade — the following applies regardless of the established credit limit:

- MLRO review of the original CDD, with documented consideration of whether the material change warrants refresh
- Refreshed customer financial information
- Refreshed sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media screening
- Documented commercial explanation of the basis for the scale-up
- Director approval before the order is accepted on credit terms
- Where the order is nevertheless accepted on the revised profile, the buyer file is updated to reflect the new commercial profile and the future credit framework is re-underwritten accordingly

This applies whether or not the absolute value of the order exceeds the established credit limit — the test is the material change from the established pattern, not the absolute value.

### 8.8 Trade credit insurance as a standing mechanism

Trade credit insurance is available to the Company not only to handle scale-up orders but as a general mechanism for extending credit to buyers where the financial information available is thin, where the trade value warrants independent underwriting, or where the buyer's risk profile otherwise benefits from a second opinion. The Company establishes a relationship with at least one reputable trade credit insurer during its first year of trading. The insurer's willingness or unwillingness to cover a buyer is itself treated as relevant risk information for the Company's decision.

### 8.9 Settlement mechanics

Settlement on credit terms is by bank transfer or by Irrevocable Documentary Letter of Credit with payment on agreed terms. The Company's standard terms are net+15 or net+30 post-delivery, specified per trade. Back-to-back Letter of Credit structures are used where appropriate — the Company holds an LC from the buyer and issues a separate, independent LC to the supplier. The Company does not use transferable LCs with document substitution.

Settlement currency is GBP on the buyer side and USD or EUR on the supplier side, dependent on origin. The Company manages foreign-exchange exposure via LC-fixed pricing, matched-term contracts, and forward cover where trade size and holding period warrant it.

## 9. Ongoing Monitoring

### 9.1 Scope

Customer Due Diligence is not a one-time exercise. All counterparties are subject to ongoing monitoring throughout the life of the relationship. Ongoing monitoring includes:

- Sanctions and adverse-media re-screening at least every six months, and immediately on any new relevant designation
- Annual refresh of CDD documentation for active relationships
- Pre-trade re-screening where more than 90 days have elapsed since the last screen
- Transaction monitoring for patterns inconsistent with the established profile of the counterparty — including unexpected changes in volume, value, product type, origin, destination, payment method, or payment routing
- Refreshed full CDD on any material change in ownership, control, or key personnel
- Trigger review on any adverse media
- Trigger review on any sanctions designation affecting the counterparty or any connected party
- Trigger review on any payment default, delay, or dispute

### 9.2 MLRO monitoring schedule

The MLRO maintains a schedule of ongoing monitoring reviews and ensures they are conducted on time. Missed reviews are treated as compliance incidents and are escalated to the Board.

## 10. Sanctions Compliance

### 10.1 Principle

The Company does not transact with any person, entity, or vessel that is subject to financial sanctions imposed by the United Kingdom, the United Nations Security Council, or the European Union, or that is owned or controlled by a sanctioned party.

### 10.2 Screening sources

At minimum:

- UK consolidated financial sanctions list (OFSI)
- UN Security Council consolidated list
- EU consolidated financial sanctions list
- Any additional regime relevant to a specific transaction (for example, US OFAC for USD-denominated trades where the Company's bank requires it)

### 10.3 Screening frequency

- At onboarding of every new counterparty
- Every six months for all active counterparties
- Immediately on becoming aware of any new designation that may be relevant
- Before any trade where more than 90 days have elapsed since the last screen

All screens are documented with date, source, and result recorded in the counterparty's file.

### 10.4 Match handling

Where a sanctions match or potential match is identified, the MLRO is notified immediately and the transaction (if any) is suspended. No further commitment is made pending the MLRO's determination. Where a match is confirmed, a report is made to OFSI without delay, and the Company complies with any licence, prohibition, or reporting requirement arising.

### 10.5 Related operational detail

Detailed operational procedure — list sources, screening tools, match-resolution workflow, record-keeping format — is set out in the separate Sanctions Screening Procedure.

## 11. No-Cash Policy

**Formal policy statement:**

Vertex Metals Ltd does not accept or make cash payments in any currency, in any amount, for any purpose in connection with its trading activities. This includes direct cash deposits into the Company's bank account, payments of cash to any third party for the Company's benefit, and the receipt of cash or cash-equivalent instruments (including cryptocurrency and any informal value transfer arrangement) as settlement for any trade.

All transaction settlements are made exclusively by:

- Bank transfer between named business accounts at regulated financial institutions, or
- Irrevocable Documentary Letter of Credit issued by an established commercial bank

This policy is communicated to all counterparties at the commencement of any business relationship and is incorporated into all contracts and terms of trade. Any counterparty request to depart from this policy is declined; any pattern of such requests is a red flag and is reported to the MLRO.

## 12. ESG and Responsible Sourcing

### 12.1 Principle

The Company sources metals, metal products, and minerals. Several materials in the Company's sourcing capability carry documented supply-chain ESG risks — including child labour in artisanal mica mining, community and environmental concerns in copper and lithium extraction, and labour standards concerns across parts of the global metals supply chain. The Company applies a documented responsible-sourcing framework.

### 12.2 Mandatory standards

- **Mica** — sourced only from suppliers holding certification under the Responsible Mica Initiative (RMI) or a demonstrably equivalent independent responsible-sourcing standard
- **Cobalt and cobalt-adjacent materials** — sourced only from suppliers with documented responsible-sourcing due diligence aligned with OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas
- **Conflict minerals (3TG — tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold)** — sourced only from suppliers with documented supply-chain due diligence
- **All other materials** — supplier ESG and labour-practices due diligence is part of the standard Supplier DD pack

### 12.3 Separate Responsible Sourcing Policy

Detailed substantive standards — including specific supplier requirements, audit expectations, and escalation procedures — are set out in the separate Supplier Code of Conduct and Responsible Sourcing Policy, which forms part of the Company's compliance framework and is referenced in all supplier contracts.

## 13. CBAM Readiness

### 13.1 Scope

The UK Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism takes effect 1 January 2027. Aluminium is within initial scope. The Company expects to be the Importer of Record for in-scope aluminium imports from India and is therefore a CBAM-obligated entity from that date, subject to the £50,000 annual threshold.

### 13.2 Operational requirements

- Supplier onboarding includes carbon-intensity enquiry and, where available, collection of third-party verified installation-level emissions data
- Suppliers are required to provide, or to demonstrate a plan to provide, verified emissions data ahead of the 1 January 2027 commencement date
- Preference is given, where commercial terms are comparable, to suppliers with lower documented carbon intensity and to suppliers already engaged with EU CBAM reporting requirements
- The Company registers as a UK CBAM authorised declarant before 1 January 2027
- CBAM liability is calculated and reported annually for the 2027 accounting period onward, with first report due 31 May 2028
- CBAM documentation is retained for five years minimum and made available to HMRC on request
- As CBAM is extended to additional materials, the Company's framework is extended accordingly

## 15. Suspicious Activity Reporting

### 15.1 What constitutes suspicion

A person acting for the Company must report to the MLRO where they know or suspect, or have reasonable grounds to know or suspect, that a person is engaged in money laundering, terrorist financing, sanctions evasion, or other financial crime. Suspicion does not require certainty — a genuine concern based on available information is sufficient.

Red flags specific to the Company's business include, without limitation:

- A counterparty unwilling or unable to provide KYC documentation, or providing documentation that appears inconsistent or falsified
- A buyer requesting payment to a third party not named in the contract
- A supplier whose bank account is in a different name or jurisdiction to the company
- Pressure to complete transactions unusually quickly without commercial justification
- Requests to split transactions in ways that appear designed to stay below reporting thresholds
- Goods or payment routing through jurisdictions with no apparent commercial rationale
- A counterparty whose described business activity does not match the nature or scale of the transaction
- Buyer-nominated supplier with unclear commercial rationale for intermediation (section 7.7)
- Scale-up order materially beyond the onboarding profile (section 8.7)
- Any sanctions or PEP match or potential match
- Any request for documentary structures intended to conceal origin, destination, ownership, or payment routing

### 15.2 Internal reporting procedure

Suspicions are reported to the MLRO promptly and in writing. The reporter must not:

- Disclose the suspicion or the fact of a report to the subject of the suspicion, or to any third party (see section 15.4 on tipping off)
- Continue to process the transaction pending the MLRO's determination
- Decline to report on grounds of uncertainty — where in doubt, report

The MLRO reviews the report within five business days and determines whether to: file a SAR with the IOM FIU or UK NCA; seek consent to proceed with the transaction where applicable; decline the relationship or transaction; or take no further action with documented reasoning.

### 15.3 External reporting

- Isle of Man FIU via THEMIS for IOM-nexus activity
- UK NCA via SAR Online for UK-nexus activity
- OFSI via the Compliance Reporting Form for sanctions matters

### 15.4 Tipping off

It is a criminal offence under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2008 (Isle of Man) and the UK Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 to disclose to a person who is the subject of a suspicion report, or to any other person, that a SAR has been made or that an investigation is being considered. All persons acting for the Company are aware of this prohibition.

## 17. Policy Review, Stress Testing, and Updates

### 17.1 Review cycle

This policy is reviewed by the MLRO and approved by the Board of Directors:

- At minimum annually
- On any material change in the Company's business model, product range, geography, or counterparty base
- On any change in applicable legislation or regulatory guidance
- Following any suspicious activity report, compliance incident, or regulatory engagement
- Following any inspection, visit, or direction from the IOM FSA or other regulatory authority

### 17.2 Pre-launch policy validation

Before the Company commences live trading, this policy is stress-tested against a set of simulated customer and supplier scenarios representative of the trading patterns the Company expects to encounter. The simulation set includes at minimum:

- First-time customer with small test order
- Customer scale-up following a successful test order, consistent with onboarding profile
- Customer scale-up materially beyond the onboarding profile
- Buyer requesting continuity with a previously-qualified supplier
- Buyer nominating a supplier not on the Approved Supplier List
- Grandfathered supplier from prior trading activity
- New supplier requiring on-site audit
- Supplier with adverse media emerging mid-relationship
- Sanctions-list match at re-screening of an active counterparty
- PEP match identified in onboarding
- Partial payment default on a credit-terms order
- Customer request for split settlement above credit limit
- Scale-up order requiring trade credit insurance
- Material ownership change at a buyer mid-relationship
- Supplier request for payment to a different bank account
- CBAM-relevant trade with a supplier unable to provide verified emissions data

Each scenario is walked through against the policy as written. Gaps, ambiguities, or unworkable requirements are documented. The policy is amended before live trading begins. The validation is repeated annually and whenever the policy is materially revised.

### 17.3 Parallel validation of supporting systems

The same scenario set is used to validate the Company's supporting systems and portal — customer onboarding workflow, credit limit tracking, supplier approval gates, sanctions screening records, approval audit trail, and document retention. A scenario that the policy handles cleanly but the systems do not support is flagged as a system gap and addressed before live trading.

### 17.4 Version history

All prior versions of this policy are retained for five years minimum. Version history is documented in the Document Control section at the head of this policy.
